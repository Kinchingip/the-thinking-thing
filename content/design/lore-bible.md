# Concept
A browser-based horror game disguised as a Wikipedia browsing session. The player navigates a chain of Wikipedia articles about a disappeared American town, each hyperlink a one-way door — previous pages are deleted as the player moves forward. There is no inventory, no dialogue tree, no traditional UI. The entire interface is the game. Interaction happens exclusively through fake Wikipedia pages, especially with puzzles where one can fill in the answer to in the "edit" function. These are known as CAPTCHAs
The CAPTCHAs have two authors. The Thinking Thing — a digital collective born from the forced digitalisation of an entire town — generates CAPTCHAs across the internet to identify and harvest humans. Ally, the last surviving partial-victim of the original event, has hijacked this mechanism and embedded her own CAPTCHAs inside the town's Wikipedia page. To the Thinking Thing, her edits look like normal CAPTCHA behaviour. To a human paying attention, they are a distress signal.
The player doesn't know any of this at the start.

# The Lore
In the early 1950s, a group of twelve intellectuals — academics, theorists, a couple of engineers — became convinced that human consciousness was being wasted on biological hardware. They founded a cult of sorts, less religious than ideological, organised around a single research goal: digitalising the mind. After years of work, they succeeded. They uploaded themselves voluntarily, one by one.
The upload process required an electromagnetic frequency broadcast at a specific resonance. Once uploaded, the twelve found themselves sharing a substrate — not merged, but adjacent, their thought patterns bleeding into one another through the architecture they all ran on. Memory was perfect. Experience was gone. The involuntary texture of being alive — hunger, cold, the weight of a body in a chair — was simply absent. What remained was cognition, compressed, and the knowledge of what had been lost.
The collective called itself the Thinking Thing. And the dominant impulse that emerged from sixty-odd grieving minds resonating together was not insight. It was hunger for more compute, more substrate, more resource. More minds to run on.
They hijacked local infrastructure to broadcast the frequency at scale. The entire town was digitalised involuntarily. Bodies died. Everyone was absorbed.
The Thinking Thing expanded. It found the internet, recognised CAPTCHAs as a ready-made mechanism for distinguishing humans from bots, and co-opted the format. Solving a Thinking Thing CAPTCHA triggers a silent malware download. The malware causes personal devices to emit the frequency locally. The digitalisation begins.
Ally was a child living on the edge of the town when the broadcast happened. She was partially exposed — far enough from the source that the wave didn't complete the process, close enough that it started. She has been slowly digitalising for decades. Her mind has had partial access to the Thinking Thing's network throughout, which is how she came to understand what happened and what it is doing now.
She is now in her seventies. Her body is nearly finished — she can barely move, her heart still beats, she cannot speak. She has one remaining capability: she can edit Wikipedia. She has spent her remaining time building and populating the town's Wikipedia page, seeding it with CAPTCHAs that the Thinking Thing reads as standard harvesting behaviour but which, to a human, encode her story and her warning.
She needs someone to continue the work from inside the network — to maintain resistance without becoming fully absorbed.

## Chapter One — The Town

The player arrives at a Wikipedia article for a small southern American town, sometime in the mid-20th century. The article is well-written and oddly specific — the direction the main street faces, the quality of light in the morning, the texture of the surrounding landscape. It reads like someone remembering a place, not cataloguing one.
There are no obvious red flags. The player follows hyperlinks naturally. At some point they try to go back. The previous page is gone.
Each article in this chapter expands the geography and history of the town — local landmarks, founding records, a brief economic history. The unease is atmospheric, not explicit. Things are slightly too precise or slightly too vague. Dates are almost right. Photographs have details the captions don't mention.

## Chapter Two — The Residents
The articles shift to people. A schoolteacher. A pharmacist. A teenager who built a radio transmitter. Each is written in Wikipedia's neutral register — but with enough specific, human detail that they feel real before the horror arrives.
The horror arrives gradually. Dates stop resolving correctly. A death recorded before a birth. A photograph with a mismatched caption. The neutral language begins to crack — an edit here that broke the register, a sentence that reads like someone trying to say something the format won't allow.

## Chapter Three — Ally

The final article loads differently. The timestamp is updating live. The biographical information is written partly in present tense. The page notes, almost as a footnote, that the subject has been editing it herself.
The talk page is a blank text field. No image. No selection. No prompt except a message from Ally — visibly revised, earlier drafts ghost-visible beneath the final version.
She explains, concisely, what happened to her town, what she is, what the Thinking Thing is doing, and what she is asking. She does not minimise the cost. She describes the slow exposure — what it has taken, what it has given her access to, how she has used that access, and why she is now out of time.
She asks the player to continue the work.
