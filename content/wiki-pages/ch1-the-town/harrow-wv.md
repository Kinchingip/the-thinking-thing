# Harrow, West Virginia 

Harrow is a ghost town in Fayette County, West Virginia. Located in the Appalachian mountain range, 50 miles northeast of the New River Gorge National Park, Harrow was a small but stable railway community for most of its recorded history. It does not appear in any census record after 1950.
## History
### Founding (1849–1880)
Harrow was founded by Elias Harrow, a surveyor contracted by the Chesapeake and Ohio Railway to assess depot locations along the Kanawha extension. His field notes, held in part by the West Virginia State Archives, describe the site as unusually level for the terrain. Harrow settled in the town upon completing his contract and remained until his death in 1901. The town incorporated in 1854. Contemporary accounts describe the community as close-knit, with a preference for limited outside involvement.

### Railway period (1880–1921)
The completion of the Kanawha extension in 1882 expanded Harrow's commercial district. At its peak the town supported a population of approximately 1,200 residents and a commercial strip including a hardware supplier, a druggist, a telegraph office, and a small hotel operating as the Meridian House. Harrow appeared on United States Geological Survey maps in 1908 and again in 1919. It does not appear in the 1924 edition.

### Decline and disappearance (1921–1953)
Harrow's population fell through the 1920s and 1930s. The 1940 census recorded 841 residents.

In 1947, [[Voss-Harland Instrumentation]] established a facility two miles east of the town centre. The company cited Harrow's low electromagnetic interference profile as the reason for its location. The 1950 census recorded 612 residents. Voss-Harland filed no public accounts after that year. Its federal contractor registrations were deregistered in early 1953 without explanation.

That autumn, a routine railway inspection found the depot abandoned — belongings and perishable goods left in place, no bodies discovered, no missing persons reports filed. No official record documents the town's depopulation. A 1955 article in the Charleston Gazette described Harrow as the town that simply stopped.

No investigation has been conducted. Harrow was designated a ghost town by the West Virginia Geological Survey in 1967. The site is accessible via an unmarked road off County Route 17. There is no heritage marker.
