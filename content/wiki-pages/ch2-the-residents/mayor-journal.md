**September 14, 1947**

The Voss-Harland people arrived yesterday morning. Three vehicles. I counted twelve of them getting out. They did not come to the town office first, as courtesy would require. George [Mather] saw them from the Courier window and came to tell me. I drove out to the facility site. A man named Harland — Curtis Harland, he said, though he offered the name without welcome — told me they had all the necessary permissions. He showed me a federal document I couldn't read properly because he didn't let me hold it.

I filed a note in the municipal records. I don't know what good it will do.

---

**March 3, 1948**

The telegraph office has been failing in strange ways. Morse says the interference pattern doesn't match anything he's seen — not lightning, not equipment fault. It comes and goes at the same time each evening, between ten and midnight. I sent a letter to the state office. I don't expect a reply.

Had dinner with George [Mather] tonight. He told me his father's paper will be sixty years old this year. Sixty years. Alfred [Mather] started the Courier in January **1888** and George has been keeping it running ever since his father passed. He said he still has the original issue somewhere. I believe him — he keeps everything.

I couldn't concentrate on the conversation. I kept thinking about the interference pattern.

---

**November 12, 1948**

I went out to the Voss-Harland facility again today, uninvited. Harland met me at the gate. He did not invite me in. I asked about the electrical interference. He said their equipment operated within all legal parameters. I asked what their equipment was. He said that information was subject to federal confidentiality requirements.

On the drive back I thought about Hausdorff. The Cantor set removes everything from an interval — takes out the middle third, then the middle thirds of what's left, and so on forever — and in the end the interval has measure zero. Nothing left of substance, but the original boundary lines still sit there looking the same. I wonder if that is what is happening here. I wonder if we are the boundary lines.

---

**July 7, 1949**

George ran that puzzle in the Courier last week. The one the anonymous reader submitted — the riddle about the guest that arrives without invitation. He said it felt right for a summer issue. I told him it made me uncomfortable and he laughed and said I had no sense of play. He is probably right.

I know what the answer to that riddle is. I don't think the author meant it innocently.

---

**February 28, 1950**

The sounds have changed. They used to be a low hum, almost inaudible, like something you feel in your teeth more than hear. Now they are slightly different. A frequency I can't name. I woke at three this morning and the room felt wrong — not that anything had moved, but that it was harder to remember that anything had moved, if that makes sense. It probably doesn't.

I sent another letter to the federal office. Fourth one. I don't believe they are read.

---

**September 19, 1950**

Something has happened. I don't know how to say what. I drove past the facility at noon and the gate was standing open — not broken, just open, as if someone had left in a hurry. I went inside. There was no one. The equipment was running. I could hear it clearly for the first time: a steady tone at a frequency that I immediately wanted to stop hearing. I left very quickly.

I could not find the twelve of them. The facility log is locked in a cabinet I didn't have a key for. I should have broken the cabinet. I didn't. I don't know why.

I am going to find George [Mather] and tell him. He will know what to do with the story, if nothing else. The Courier may be the only record that gets out.
