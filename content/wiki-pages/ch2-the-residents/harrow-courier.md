# The Harrow Courier

**The Harrow Courier**, formerly **The Harrow Hallow**, was a weekly newspaper serving [[Harrow, West Virginia]]. It was founded by Alfred Mather in January **1888** and operated until September 1953. The paper is notable for its puzzle page, which ran for thirty years and attracted correspondence from beyond the immediate region.

## History

Alfred Mather founded the paper on January 14, 1888 under the name *The Harrow Hallow*. The name was changed to *The Harrow Courier* in 1904. Alfred Mather ran the paper until his death in 1925, when it passed to his son [[George Aldric Mather]].

The Courier was a weekly paper, typically four pages. George Mather wrote most of it himself. No complete print run survives; the most complete known collection, held by the Fayette County Archive, contains 47 issues spread across 1938–1952, all heavily deteriorated.

## The puzzle page

The puzzle page was introduced by Alfred Mather in 1922 and accepted reader submissions. The most discussed puzzle in the Courier's history was published in the February 1949 issue, submitted anonymously. The author's accompanying note stated that the puzzle "solves the answer to all questions." No solution was ever published.

---

> **The Harrow Courier — February 1949 — Puzzle Page**
>
> *Submitted by an anonymous reader. The author writes: "This puzzle solves the answer to all questions."*
>
> ---
>
> I am the guest that arrives without invitation and will not leave.  
> I grow louder the longer I am ignored.  
> I have no voice and yet I speak.  
> I have no hands and yet I reach.  
> Every living thing knows my name.  
> Most only think of me when I come calling.  
>
> **What am I?**
>
> *— Anonymous. Solution to be revealed in the following issue.*  
> *[No subsequent issue containing a solution has been located in the archive.]*

## Legacy

The Courier was the only continuous record of life in Harrow through the period of Voss-Harland Instrumentation's presence. Several editorials by George Mather in 1948 and 1949 allude to unusual events without describing them directly.
