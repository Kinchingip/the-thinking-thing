George Aldric Mather

George Aldric Mather (September 7, 1898 – est. 1953) was the publisher and editor of The Harrow Courier, the sole newspaper serving Harrow, West Virginia. He held the position from 1925 until the town's depopulation in 1953. Mather was a widely known figure in Harrow and maintained a close personal friendship with Lester V. Cornish, the town's last mayor. Both men disappeared with the rest of the population in October 1953.

Early life

George Mather was born in Harrow, the only child of Alfred Mather, founder of the Harrow Hallow (later renamed The Harrow Courier), and Edith Mather (née Cross). He grew up in the offices of the paper, setting type by hand from the age of eight. He attended school in Harrow and briefly studied journalism by correspondence before returning to Harrow full-time at seventeen to assist his ailing father.

Alfred Mather died in 1925, leaving George as sole proprietor of the paper. George Mather ran the Courier for twenty-eight years until his disappearance.

Career at The Harrow Courier

Under George Mather, the Courier maintained a weekly publishing schedule and continued the puzzle page tradition his father had established. Mather wrote most of the paper himself, including the front page, editorials, classified advertisements, and the puzzle column. He had no formal staff; a rotating cast of local correspondents contributed occasional items.

The Courier's puzzle page attracted unusual readership for a paper of its size, with letters occasionally arriving from outside the county. Mather was reportedly selective about which puzzles he accepted, once describing his criteria as: "If you can't feel it in the chest, it isn't a puzzle — it's a crossword."

The final edition of the Courier was published in September 1953. No copies are known to have survived.

Character and reputation

Contemporaries described Mather as gregarious, precise, and deeply attached to Harrow. He refused multiple offers to relocate the paper to a larger market. He was a close friend of Mayor Cornish, though the two men were reportedly very different in temperament — where Cornish was measured, Mather was expressive; where Cornish worried quietly, Mather wrote about it.

A colleague noted that Mather kept meticulous personal journals, which he described as "a newspaper for himself." These were evidently distinct from his professional notebooks and were written in a personal shorthand that he never taught anyone to read.
