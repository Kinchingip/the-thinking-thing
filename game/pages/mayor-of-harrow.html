Lester V. Cornish

Lester Vernon Cornish (March 2, 1901 – est. 1953) served as Mayor of Harrow, West Virginia from 1939 until the town's depopulation in 1953. He was the last person to hold the office. Cornish is notable as the only municipal official on record who submitted formal written complaints to state and federal authorities regarding the activities of Voss-Harland Instrumentation. All complaints were declined without review.

Early life and education

Cornish was born in Harrow, the second child of a telegraph operator and a schoolteacher. He attended Harrow's local schoolhouse through the age of fourteen, then completed his secondary education by correspondence. He was largely self-educated thereafter, a fact he cited with some pride in the few extant public statements attributed to him.

His private reading was extensive and eclectic. Acquaintances noted that he maintained a personal library of unusual scope for a small-town official. Among the volumes described in accounts of his study was a well-worn copy of Felix Hausdorff's Set Theory (1914, German edition), which he reportedly consulted throughout his tenure. He told at least one visitor that he found the concept of measure useful for thinking about what things actually contained, as opposed to what they appeared to contain.

Political career

Cornish was elected mayor in 1939 and re-elected without opposition in 1943 and 1947. The role was largely administrative in a town of Harrow's size; he oversaw road maintenance, the school, and the limited commercial licensing on Meridian Street.

He is reported to have been a methodical administrator who kept detailed records of municipal decisions. These records have not been located.

Response to Voss-Harland Instrumentation

Cornish filed at least four formal complaints regarding Voss-Harland Instrumentation between 1948 and 1950, citing noise disturbances, unexplained electrical interference with the town's telegraph system, and what he described in one document as "an escalating pattern of behaviour inconsistent with the stated research purpose." The complaints were addressed to the West Virginia Office of the Governor, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, and the Department of Defense. All were returned without action.

His private record of this period is believed to survive in the form of personal journals, though access to these materials has historically been restricted by the archive holding them. [1]

Personal life

Cornish never married. He lived in the same house on Meridian Street in which he was born. He was described by contemporaries as reserved, precise in speech, and uncomfortable with uncertainty. He was known to keep meticulous personal accounts, including a journal he had maintained since the age of seventeen. A colleague recalled that Cornish once said: "The only thing worse than not understanding something is convincing yourself that you do."

He disappeared with the rest of Harrow's population in 1953. No remains were recovered. He was 52 years old.
