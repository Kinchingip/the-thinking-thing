The Harrow Courier

The Harrow Courier, formerly The Harrow Hallow, was a weekly newspaper serving Harrow, West Virginia. It was founded by Alfred Mather in January 1888 and operated continuously until September 1953, when publication ceased following the depopulation of Harrow. The paper is notable for its puzzle page, which ran for thirty years and attracted correspondence from beyond the immediate region.

History

Alfred Mather founded the paper in January 1888 under the name The Harrow Hallow. The first issue was printed on January 14 of that year. The name was changed to The Harrow Courier in 1904, apparently for reasons of commercial legibility — Alfred Mather's private notebooks, partially preserved, note only that "the old name was misread too often."

Alfred Mather ran the paper until his death in 1925, when it passed to his son, George Aldric Mather, who continued publication until the paper's final issue in September 1953.

The Courier was a weekly paper, typically four pages, covering local news, commercial notices, and community correspondence. George Mather wrote most of it himself. No complete print run of the Courier survives; the most complete known collection, held by the Fayette County Archive, contains 47 issues spread across 1938–1952, all heavily deteriorated.

The puzzle page

The puzzle page was introduced by Alfred Mather in 1922 and maintained by George Mather throughout his tenure. It accepted reader submissions. Puzzles ranged from word games and logic problems to more philosophical riddles, the latter apparently a preference of George Mather's. The page attracted a small but dedicated correspondence from outside Fayette County.

The most discussed puzzle in the Courier's history was published in the February 1949 issue, submitted anonymously. The author's accompanying note stated that the puzzle "solves the answer to all questions." No solution was ever published. No letters responding to it are preserved in the archive.

The Harrow Courier February 1949 — Puzzle Page

Submitted by an anonymous reader. The author writes: "This puzzle solves the answer to all questions."

I am the guest that arrives without invitation and will not leave.

I grow louder the longer I am ignored.

I have no voice and yet I speak.

I have no hands and yet I reach.

Every living thing knows my name.

Most only think of me when I come calling.

What am I?

— Anonymous. Solution to be revealed in the following issue.

[No subsequent issue containing a solution has been located in the archive.]

Legacy

The Courier was the only continuous record of life in Harrow through the period of Voss-Harland Instrumentation's presence in the town. The surviving issues are the primary source for scholars studying the Harrow disappearance. Several editorials by George Mather in 1948 and 1949 allude to unusual events in the town without describing them directly — a restraint that has been interpreted variously as journalistic caution, self-censorship, and, in at least one academic paper, evidence of interference in the editorial process.

The final issue of the Courier, September 1953, reported on routine local matters without any reference to the events that would shortly follow. It is not known whether George Mather was aware, at the time of writing, of what was coming.
